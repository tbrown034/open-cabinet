# The Executive Branch Divestiture Process: A Research Brief

## How Ethics Agreements Work, Who Enforces Them, and Why Compliance Is Failing

*Research compiled April 12, 2026 | For Open Cabinet internal knowledge base*

---

## 1. The Ethics Agreement Process

### What It Is

An ethics agreement is any oral or written promise by a reporting individual to undertake specific actions to alleviate an actual or apparent conflict of interest. It is the primary mechanism through which incoming executive branch officials resolve financial conflicts before assuming their duties.

The legal definition comes from **5 CFR 2634.802(a)**, which specifies that permitted actions include: divesting financial interests, recusing from certain matters, resigning from non-federal positions, obtaining a waiver under 18 U.S.C. 208(b), or establishing a qualified blind or diversified trust.

### When It Happens

The ethics agreement is negotiated **before Senate confirmation**, during the vetting process. The timeline works as follows:

1. **Nomination announced.** The White House vetting team begins reviewing the nominee's financial background.
2. **Financial disclosure filed.** The nominee completes OGE Form 278e (Public Financial Disclosure Report), listing all assets, income, liabilities, and outside positions.
3. **Review and negotiation.** The agency's Designated Agency Ethics Official (DAEO), in collaboration with OGE, reviews the disclosure, identifies potential conflicts under 18 U.S.C. 208, and works with the nominee to develop remedies. This is the negotiation phase. (Source: [Congressional Research Service, IF12736](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12736))
4. **Ethics agreement signed.** The nominee signs a written agreement specifying exactly what will be divested, by when, and what recusals will apply in the interim.
5. **Senate receives the agreement.** The ethics agreement is transmitted to the relevant Senate confirmation committee alongside the financial disclosure. Senators can and do reference it during confirmation hearings.
6. **Confirmation vote.** The Senate votes with the ethics agreement as part of the record.

### Who Negotiates

Three parties are involved:

- **The nominee**, who proposes which assets to divest and which to retain
- **The Designated Agency Ethics Official (DAEO)**, the officer designated by the head of each agency to coordinate and manage the agency's ethics program (defined in 5 CFR 2638.603)
- **OGE**, which reviews the agreement for Senate-confirmed Presidential Appointees (PAS positions) and must concur before the agreement is finalized

OGE's role is advisory and review-based. It does not have direct enforcement authority. Rather, individual agencies, through the DAEO, are responsible for enforcing required divestitures and reviewing compliance. (Source: [5 CFR Part 2638](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-5/chapter-XVI/subchapter-B/part-2638))

## 2. The 90-Day Divestiture Window

### The Rule

Under **5 CFR 2634.802(b)**, an individual must complete all actions specified in their ethics agreement "within a period not to exceed three months from the date of the agreement (or of Senate confirmation, if applicable)."

This is not a STOCK Act requirement. The STOCK Act (Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act of 2012) primarily addresses insider trading and periodic transaction reporting. The 90-day divestiture deadline comes from the **ethics agreement itself**, enforced under OGE's regulatory framework.

### How It Works in Practice

- **Day 0:** Senate confirms the nominee.
- **Day 90:** All divestitures, resignations, and other actions specified in the ethics agreement must be completed.
- **Day 97:** The DAEO must submit a Certification of Ethics Agreement Compliance to OGE, within seven days of the compliance deadline. (Source: [OGE Program Advisory, May 2017](https://www.oge.gov/web/oge.nsf/News+Releases/DC3E62B16037AC69852585BA005BEC44/$FILE/PROGRAM%20ADVISORY%20AND%20EA%20CERTIFICATION%20(May%202017).pdf))

If the ethics agreement specifies a longer period for a particular provision (e.g., an illiquid asset that takes longer to sell), the individual must still comply with all other provisions within three months and submit partial certification.

### How It's Enforced

Poorly.

OGE has no enforcement authority. It can write letters to Senate committees. It can publicly post compliance certifications (or the lack thereof) on its website. It can refer matters to the Department of Justice for potential criminal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. 208 (the criminal conflict of interest statute). But it cannot impose fines, revoke appointments, or compel action.

The actual enforcement chain runs: **DAEO notifies OGE of non-compliance -> OGE notifies Senate committee -> Senate can hold hearings or apply political pressure -> DOJ can theoretically prosecute.** In practice, DOJ prosecution for missed divestiture deadlines is exceedingly rare to nonexistent.

## 3. What Must Be Divested

### The Conflict Standard

The governing statute is **18 U.S.C. 208**, which makes it a criminal offense for an executive branch employee to participate "personally and substantially in a particular matter in which, to his knowledge, he, his spouse, minor child, general partner, organization in which he is serving as officer, director, trustee, general partner or employee, or any person or organization with whom he is negotiating or has any arrangement concerning prospective employment, has a financial interest."

Not everything must be divested. Only holdings that create a **conflict with the official's specific duties** trigger the requirement. The determination of what conflicts is made by the DAEO in consultation with OGE, based on the official's job responsibilities and the nature of their financial holdings.

### What Typically Must Go

- **Individual stocks in companies regulated by the official's agency.** An Energy Secretary holding oil company stock. A Treasury Secretary holding bank stock. An FCC chair holding telecom stock.
- **Ownership interests in companies that do business with the agency.** This includes stock, partnership interests, and equity stakes.
- **Outside positions** with entities that interact with the agency. Board seats, consulting arrangements, advisory roles.

### What Can Usually Be Kept

- **Diversified mutual funds and ETFs.** Under 5 CFR 2640.201(a), employees are exempt from the conflict of interest prohibition for financial interests held in diversified funds. This is why the Certificate of Divestiture program steers proceeds into diversified funds.
- **U.S. Treasury securities.** Government bonds have no conflict potential.
- **Real estate** (primary residence and rental properties) unless the agency directly regulates real estate.
- **Retirement accounts** in diversified funds.

### The Gray Areas

The determination of "conflict" involves judgment calls. An official might argue that a holding is too small to matter, or that their duties don't overlap with the company's regulated activities. Ethics officials can grant **waivers under 18 U.S.C. 208(b)(1)** if the financial interest is "not so substantial as to be deemed likely to affect the integrity of the services which the Government may expect." These waivers are individually negotiated.

## 4. Certificate of Divestiture (26 U.S.C. 1043)

### The Purpose

The Certificate of Divestiture (CD) is a tax benefit designed to make compliance less financially painful. Without it, an official forced to sell $500 million in appreciated stock would face an immediate capital gains tax bill of roughly $100 million or more. That's a powerful disincentive to enter government service.

Congress addressed this in **26 U.S.C. 1043**, originally enacted in 1989, which allows eligible persons to **defer capital gains tax** on property sold to comply with conflict-of-interest requirements.

### How It Works

1. **Before selling**, the official requests a Certificate of Divestiture from OGE through their DAEO.
2. OGE reviews the request and determines whether the divestiture is "reasonably necessary to comply with any Federal conflict of interest statute, regulation, rule, or executive order." (Source: [5 CFR 2634.1001](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/5/2634.1001))
3. OGE issues the certificate, signed by the OGE Director or General Counsel.
4. The official sells the conflicting asset.
5. **Within 60 days** of the sale, the official must reinvest the proceeds into "permitted property."
6. Capital gains tax is **deferred** until the permitted property is eventually sold.

### Permitted Property

Permitted property is narrowly defined as:
- **U.S. government obligations** (Treasury bonds, bills, notes)
- **Diversified investment funds** approved by OGE regulations (mutual funds or ETFs that do not concentrate in any single industry, business, or country other than the U.S.)

(Source: [26 U.S.C. 1043(b)(4)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1043); [OGE Certificate of Divestiture Fact Sheet](https://www.oge.gov/web/OGE.nsf/0/D9246BEE2437F154852585B6005A17D1/$FILE/Certificate%20of%20Divestiture%20Fact%20Sheet.pdf))

### Why It Incentivizes Compliance

The tax deferral is substantial. Rex Tillerson saved an estimated **$72 million** in immediate federal income tax by using a Certificate of Divestiture when he sold his ExxonMobil holdings in 2017. Henry Paulson saved approximately **$50 million** when he divested over $490 million in Goldman Sachs stock in 2006. Frank Bisignano's sales of approximately $530-560 million in Fiserv stock in 2025 would have generated enormous capital gains without the certificate.

The critical detail: **the certificate must be obtained before the sale.** An official who divests without first getting a CD loses the tax benefit entirely. This creates an incentive to work with OGE promptly. (Source: [5 CFR 2634.1005](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/5/2634.1005))

### The Criticism

Critics note that the Certificate of Divestiture effectively rewards wealthy appointees for entering government. An official who might otherwise owe $100 million in capital gains gets to defer that tax indefinitely by rolling proceeds into Treasury bonds. Senator Tammy Baldwin called Tillerson's arrangement a "government service golden parachute." (Source: [PolitiFact, Feb 2017](https://www.politifact.com/factchecks/2017/feb/10/tammy-baldwin/secretary-state-rex-tillersons-180-million-exxon-g/))

Biden cabinet members also used the mechanism. Fox News reported that multiple Biden cabinet officials received certificates of divestiture to avoid capital gains hits. (Source: [Fox News, Biden Cabinet Certificates](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/biden-cabinet-certificates-of-divestiture))

The benefit is bipartisan and structural. Whether the official is a Democrat or Republican, 26 U.S.C. 1043 works the same way.

## 5. Extensions

### The Legal Standard

Under **5 CFR 2634.802(b)**, the three-month compliance deadline can be extended in cases of **"unusual hardship,"** as determined by OGE (for agreements it reviews) or by the DAEO (for all other ethics agreements).

The regulation does not define "unusual hardship" with specificity. It provides one illustrative example: an SEC officer or director subject to Section 16 restrictions that prevent stock sales within a six-month window. In that case, the conflict between federal ethics deadlines and securities law creates a legitimate hardship.

### Amendment vs. Extension

Ethics agreements can also be **amended** after execution, but this requires a higher bar. OGE considers amendment requests only if there is "a change in law or material change in circumstances outside the nominee's control." (Source: Senate Commerce Committee, December 2025, re: Bedford case)

In the Bryan Bedford case (discussed below), the FAA administrator sought to amend his ethics agreement after his 90-day deadline had passed. OGE denied the amendment, stating it "did not meet the standard for granting an amendment."

### How Common Are Extensions?

There is no published data on how frequently extensions are granted. OGE does not release aggregate statistics. What the public record shows is that extensions have become **more visible** in 2025-2026 than in prior administrations, with multiple high-profile cases hitting the news:

- **Scott Bessent** received an extension to December 15, 2025, after missing his April 28 deadline.
- **Bryan Bedford** was denied an amendment request and remained in violation for 150+ days.
- [NEEDS VERIFICATION] It is unclear how many other current administration officials have quietly received extensions without public disclosure.

## 6. Notable Divestiture Cases (2025-2026 Administration)

### Scott Bessent — Treasury Secretary

**The facts:** Bessent was confirmed January 27, 2025. His ethics agreement, signed January 9, 2025, committed him to divesting interests in 25 entities within 90 days. That put his deadline at approximately April 28, 2025.

**What went wrong:** By August 2025, OGE Deputy Compliance Director Dale Christopher wrote to Senate Finance Committee Chair Mike Crapo that Bessent "has failed to timely comply with certain terms of the ethics agreement he signed." The primary holdout: up to $25 million in soybean and corn farmland across thousands of acres in five North Dakota counties, generating up to $1 million per year in rental income. Treasury ethics officials described the assets as "illiquid and not readily marketable."

**Resolution:** OGE, Bessent, and Treasury ethics officials mutually agreed to an extension until December 15, 2025. Bessent stated he had completed 96% of required divestitures. On December 7, 2025, appearing on CBS's *Face the Nation*, Bessent announced he had divested his soybean farm interests, completing compliance approximately **10 months** after confirmation.

(Sources: [CNBC, Dec 7 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/07/treasury-secretary-bessent-divested-soybean-farm.html); [U.S. News, Aug 13 2025](https://www.usnews.com/news/politics/articles/2025-08-13/us-treasurys-bessent-not-in-compliance-with-ethics-agreement); [Banking Dive](https://www.bankingdive.com/news/bessent-ethics-farmland-divest-assets-investments-china-north-dakota-treasury-crapo-wyden-oge/757703/))

### Howard Lutnick — Commerce Secretary

**The facts:** Lutnick's ethics agreement was dated January 21, 2025. He committed to divesting his ownership, voting, and economic interests in Cantor Fitzgerald, BGC Group, and Newmark Group. He stepped down from his positions at all three firms upon confirmation.

**Divestiture transactions:** BGC Group repurchased 16,452,850 shares of Class A common stock from Lutnick at $9.2082 per share (volume-weighted average price from May 14-16, 2025). Lutnick entered agreements to forgo all economic benefits in BGC as of May 16, 2025.

**Complication — Tether:** Lutnick sold his multibillion-dollar ownership interest in Cantor Fitzgerald to trusts benefiting his four children in October 2024. Around the same time, one of those trusts borrowed an undisclosed sum from Tether, the stablecoin issuer for which Cantor Fitzgerald serves as primary U.S. Treasury custodian. Ethics experts flagged that while the transaction may formally comply with rules, it "in substance creates a new problem" by linking the Lutnick family's financial interests to a company that could be affected by Commerce Department crypto policy. (Source: [Union-Bulletin/AP](https://www.union-bulletin.com/news/national/as-lutnick-sold-cantor-to-his-children-tether-gave-them-a-loan/article_fd10a016-9fb8-5714-9a6e-75d746885948.html); [Salon, April 2025](https://www.salon.com/2025/04/24/a-5-star-rating-for-conflicts-of-interest-commerce-sec-howard-lutnicks-finances-raise-red-flags/))

**Compliance status:** A Commerce Department spokesperson stated that Lutnick "has fully complied with the terms of his ethics agreement with respect to divestiture and recusals."

(Sources: [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/howard-lutnick-divest-corporate-holdings-commerce-secretary/); [BusinessWire, May 2025](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20250519432705/en/BGC-Announces-Repurchase-of-More-Than-16.4-Million-Shares-from-Howard-W.-Lutnick-United-States-Secretary-of-Commerce-the-Companys-Former-Chairman-and-CEO))

### Chris Wright — Energy Secretary

**The facts:** Wright was confirmed February 3, 2025. His ethics agreement was dated January 14, 2025. He held nearly 3 million shares of Liberty Energy, the fracking firm he founded in 2011, worth over $53 million as of mid-February. He also held 63,459 common units of Energy Transfer, the pipeline company co-founded by major Trump donor Kelcy Warren, plus stakes in eight other energy companies.

**Divestiture:** OGE issued a Certificate of Divestiture on February 18, 2025, signed by OGE General Counsel David Apol, authorizing the sale of Wright's energy company holdings. This was notably fast — just 15 days after confirmation.

**Complication:** Wright's ethics agreement included provisions allowing continued receipt of a March 2025 bonus from Liberty Energy. Until receiving payment, he committed to not participating in matters directly affecting Liberty's ability to make that payment, unless obtaining a written waiver.

(Sources: [E&E News by POLITICO, Feb 24 2025](https://www.eenews.net/articles/details-emerge-on-wrights-stock-holdings-doe-firings/); [Wright Ethics Agreement](https://wallstreetonparade.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/Chris-Wright-Nominee-for-Energy-Secretary-Ethics-Agreement.pdf))

### Frank Bisignano — SSA Commissioner

**The facts:** Bisignano, former CEO of Fiserv, was confirmed in May 2025 to lead the Social Security Administration. He was required to divest all Fiserv holdings. He sold approximately $530-560 million in Fiserv stock between May and August 2025, using tax-advantaged sales on May 16 and July 1, 2025, through Certificates of Divestiture.

**The controversy:** In October 2025, Fiserv's new CEO Mike Lyons withdrew earnings forecasts that Bisignano had set, calling them "objectively difficult to achieve." Fiserv stock fell more than 40%, erasing approximately $30 billion in market value. Because Bisignano had already sold, he avoided an estimated $354 million in losses.

**Congressional response:** Rep. John B. Larson and Rep. Jim Himes referred Bisignano to the SEC for investigation into whether the timing of his stock sales reflected foreknowledge of the coming earnings problems. Senators Ron Wyden and Elizabeth Warren launched a separate investigation into the stock sell-off and Fiserv's federal contract relationship with SSA. The administration maintained that Bisignano "fully complied with all obligations required under his federal government ethics agreement."

(Sources: [Rep. Larson press release](https://larson.house.gov/media-center/press-releases/larson-himes-refer-social-security-commissioner-frank-bisignano-sec); [Government Executive, Oct 2025](https://www.govexec.com/oversight/2025/10/bisignano-draws-scrutiny-his-former-company-falters/409196/); [Government Executive, Nov 2025](https://www.govexec.com/oversight/2025/11/ssa-chief-bisignano-faces-probe-wyden-and-warren-over-fiserv-stock-and-federal-contract-concerns/409402/))

**The facts:** McMahon was confirmed March 3, 2025. Her financial disclosure revealed over $50 million in TKO Group Holdings stock (the Endeavor entity formed from the WWE-UFC merger). She signed an ethics waiver allowing participation in the America 250 Civics Education Coalition despite her prior role at America First Policy Institute.

**Compliance status:** [NEEDS VERIFICATION] Specific details of McMahon's divestiture commitments and compliance timeline have not been widely reported. Her ethics agreement likely focused more on recusals related to AFPI than on stock divestiture.

### Bryan Bedford — FAA Administrator

**The facts:** Bedford signed his ethics agreement June 4, 2025, and was confirmed July 9, 2025. He reported holding Republic Airways stock worth between $6 million and $30 million. He pledged to divest "as soon as practicable but not later than 90 days after my confirmation," setting a deadline of approximately October 9, 2025.

**What went wrong:** Bedford did not divest. As of September 11, 2025, he still held 16,733 Republic shares. He sought to amend his ethics agreement — an unusual post-confirmation step — but OGE denied the request because it "did not meet the standard for granting an amendment." The Department of Transportation ethics office "expressed confusion" about the amendment's status.

**The merger angle:** On November 25, 2025, Republic Airways completed a merger with Mesa Air Group. Republic shareholders owned approximately 88% of the combined entity. Bedford's failure to divest before the merger announcement meant he potentially benefited from the value increase. As of OGE's December 8, 2025, letter to the Commerce Committee, Bedford had been in violation for over 150 days.

(Sources: [Senate Commerce Committee, Dec 2025](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/12/faa-administrator-bedford-in-violation-of-ethics-agreement-potentially-holds-millions-in-republic-airways-stock-months-after-confirmation-committee); [Senate Commerce Committee, Feb 2026](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2026/2/bedford-s-violation-of-ethics-agreement-may-yield-the-sitting-faa-chief-millions))

### Elon Musk — DOGE (Special Government Employee)

**The unusual case:** Musk served as head of the Department of Government Efficiency as a "special government employee," a classification that makes him subject to 18 U.S.C. 208 but with different disclosure requirements than Senate-confirmed appointees. His companies — Tesla, SpaceX, Starlink, and others — hold at least $15.4 billion in government contracts over the past decade and have been the subject of at least 20 federal investigations or reviews.

The White House stated Musk would self-recuse when conflicts arise. No public ethics agreement was filed. Senator Adam Schiff demanded answers from OGE about Musk's compliance. Senator Elizabeth Warren said the arrangement was "an invitation for corruption on a scale not seen in our lifetimes."

(Sources: [Fortune, Feb 2025](https://fortune.com/2025/02/06/elon-musk-conflicts-interest-doge-tesla-spacex/); [Sen. ## 7. Historical Comparisons

### Bush Administration (2001-2009)

**Henry Paulson** set the modern benchmark for high-value divestitures. When he became Treasury Secretary in 2006, he was required to sell over **$490 million in Goldman Sachs stock** and placed at least $375 million in additional assets into a blind trust. He received a Certificate of Divestiture that deferred approximately $50 million in capital gains taxes. (Sources: [Slate, June 2006](https://slate.com/business/2006/06/henry-paulson-s-conflict-of-interest.html); [CNN Business, Jan 2017](https://edition.cnn.com/2017/01/10/news/tillerson-tax-break/index.html))

### Obama Administration (2009-2017)

The Biden-era CREW report noted that the Obama-Biden framework established a two-year post-government lobbying ban (vs. Trump's one-year ban). CREW's reviews of Biden-era officials — who largely followed the Obama model — found "a high degree of compliance with divestiture obligations, and even includes examples of cabinet members going beyond required divestitures." The vast majority of Biden's cabinet either held no publicly traded stock or divested entirely. (Source: [CREW, Biden Financial Disclosures](https://www.citizensforethics.org/reports-investigations/crew-reports/biden-administration-financial-disclosures-show-strong-pattern-of-compliance/))

### Trump First Term (2017-2021)

**Rex Tillerson** divested his entire ExxonMobil position through a $180 million package. ExxonMobil bought back his unvested shares and placed the cash in a blind trust paying out over 10 years, with a clawback provision: if Tillerson returned to the oil and gas industry within 10 years, the money would go to charity. The structure deferred approximately $72 million in capital gains taxes. Tillerson's divestiture was considered complete and compliant. (Sources: [CNBC, March 2018](https://www.cnbc.com/2018/03/13/rex-tillersons-tax-deal-may-have-made-year-in-trumps-orbit-worth-it.html); [Fortune, Jan 2017](https://fortune.com/2017/01/04/donald-trump-rex-tillerson-exxon-mobil-180-million/))

**Wilbur Ross** was the cautionary tale. CREW filed criminal and ethics complaints against the Commerce Secretary. Ross failed to divest over $10 million in Invesco stock despite submitting a sworn statement that he had divested everything required. Separately, he took a short position in Navigator Holdings — a company connected to Russian interests through the Paradise Papers — the day before a New York Times story about his ties to the company was published. An inspector general investigation cleared him of insider trading but found he violated the general standard against creating the appearance of violating the law. (Sources: [CREW](https://www.citizensforethics.org/news/press-releases/wilbur-ross/); [CNBC, June 2018](https://www.cnbc.com/2018/06/18/commerce-chief-wilbur-ross-financial-holdings-eyed-in-report.html); [Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/update/wilbur-ross-violated-ethics-rules-multi-year-investigation-clc-complaint-finds))

CREW's overall assessment of the first Trump term: "Like President Trump, his cabinet members and senior White House officials seemingly disregarded their ethical obligations, including the possible conflicts of interest that could arise from the business interests they retained." (Source: [CREW](https://www.citizensforethics.org/reports-investigations/crew-reports/biden-administration-financial-disclosures-show-strong-pattern-of-compliance/))

### Biden Administration (2021-2025)

CREW's review of 2022 financial disclosures found "a high degree of compliance with divestiture obligations." Biden's cabinet members were required to file annual reports by May 16, 2022. OGE reviewed and certified all reports by November 17, 2022. CREW found that most high-level officials "observed ethical best practices in divesting themselves from assets that could conflict with their official duties." Biden's executive ethics order also reinstated the two-year post-government lobbying ban that Obama had used and Trump had reduced to one year. (Source: [CREW, Biden Cabinet Overview](https://www.citizensforethics.org/reports-investigations/crew-reports/an-overview-of-bidens-cabinet-members-handling-of-financial-conflicts-of-interest-issues/))

## 8. Blind Trusts

### How They Work

A qualified blind trust is an arrangement where a government official transfers assets to an independent trustee, who manages them without the official's knowledge or input. The official does not know what the trustee buys, sells, or holds. In theory, this eliminates conflicts of interest because the official cannot be influenced by holdings they don't know about.

### OGE Certification

Under **5 U.S.C. 13104(f)** and **5 CFR Part 2634, Subpart D**, a trust is not recognized as a qualified blind trust for any purpose under federal law unless it has been **certified by OGE prior to its execution**. The trust agreement must adhere to OGE's model trust documents, with variations permitted only with OGE's agreement for unusual circumstances.

OGE maintains 12 model certificates and trust documents. The requirements are extensive: the trustee must be independent (typically a bank or financial institution), the official cannot communicate with the trustee about investments, the trustee cannot inform the official about holdings, and the trust must be structured so that the official genuinely does not know what is in it. (Source: [OGE Qualified Trusts page](https://www.oge.gov/Web/oge.nsf/Resources/Qualified+Trusts))

### How Rare They Are

Qualified blind trusts are **extremely rare** in practice. According to OGE's own estimates in federal paperwork reduction filings:

- There are **no active qualified trusts** in the executive branch as of 2025.
- Historical usage involved approximately 10 qualified blind trusts and 15 qualified diversified trusts total across the executive branch.
- OGE estimates an average of **2 new qualified trust applications per year**.

(Source: [Federal Register, Aug 2025](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/08/08/2025-15116/agency-information-collection-activities-information-collection-renewal-comment-request-for); [CRS, RS21656](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/RS21656.html))

### Why So Few?

Several factors explain the rarity:

1. **Cost.** Setting up and maintaining a qualified blind trust is expensive. Trustee fees, legal costs, and administrative overhead can run into hundreds of thousands of dollars annually.
2. **Complexity.** OGE's model documents are extensive and the certification process is rigorous.
3. **Divestiture is simpler.** For most officials, selling conflicting assets (with the 1043 tax benefit) is faster and cheaper than establishing a blind trust.
4. **Blind trusts don't solve all problems.** If an official knows what was in the trust when it was established (and they always do, since they transferred the assets), the "blindness" is imperfect for assets held at inception. New investments by the trustee would be truly blind, but legacy holdings are known.
5. **Political optics.** Critics argue that blind trusts are performative — the official knows the general composition of their wealth even if they don't know daily trades. This has made the mechanism less politically attractive than full divestiture.

### Historical Usage

The most prominent recent use of a trust arrangement (though not a standard OGE-certified blind trust) was **Rex Tillerson's cash trust** in 2017, where ExxonMobil placed $180 million in a trust invested in Treasury-like securities, overseen by an independent trustee, with a 10-year payout schedule.

President Trump during both terms chose **not** to use a blind trust, instead maintaining his business empire under the management of his sons. Ethics experts criticized this arrangement as inadequate, since Trump knew exactly what his businesses were and could be influenced by policies affecting them. (Source: [San.com](https://san.com/cc/does-trump-have-to-sell-his-djt-stake-we-asked-a-white-house-ethics-lawyer/))

## 9. The Enforcement Gap in 2025-2026

### OGE Leadership Instability

On **February 10, 2025**, President Trump removed OGE Director David Huitema — who had been confirmed by the Senate just two months earlier in a lame-duck session vote of 50-46. Huitema said his sense was that "the president doesn't want OGE or really anyone with an independent voice to address concerns that are raised."

Trump named Veterans Affairs Secretary Doug Collins as interim head. By August 2025, OMB Deputy Director Eric Ueland was the fourth person to hold the OGE top position in a single year. CREW called this "unprecedented meddling" that "turned OGE into a revolving door."

(Sources: [CNN, Feb 2025](https://www.cnn.com/2025/02/10/politics/office-of-government-ethics-david-huitema-trump); [CREW](https://www.citizensforethics.org/reports-investigations/crew-investigations/trumps-unprecedented-meddling-has-turned-oge-into-a-revolving-door/); [Common Cause](https://www.commoncause.org/resources/trump-weakens-government-oversight/))

### The Pattern

Across the current administration, a pattern has emerged:

- **Bessent** missed his deadline by months, received an extension, and finally completed compliance in December 2025 — 10 months after confirmation.
- **Bedford** missed his deadline, was denied an amendment, and remained in violation for 150+ days while potentially benefiting from a merger.
- **Bisignano** technically complied with his divestiture timeline but is now under SEC investigation for the timing of sales relative to earnings problems at his former company.
- **Musk** operated with billions in government contracts and no public ethics agreement.
- **OGE** cycled through four leaders in a single year, undermining institutional independence.

The enforcement tools available — letters to Senate committees, public disclosure, DOJ referrals — appear insufficient when the political will to enforce them is absent. As the Campaign Legal Center put it: "The lack of ethics enforcement in government provides the blueprint for reform." (Source: [Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/update/lack-ethics-enforcement-government-provides-blueprint-reform))
