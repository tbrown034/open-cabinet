## 1. The STOCK Act: Legislative History

### Origins

The Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act was first introduced on March 28, 2006, by Rep. Louise Slaughter (D-NY) and Rep. Brian Baird (D-WA). The bill was a direct response to insider trading allegations against Tony Rudy, a top aide to then-House Majority Leader Tom DeLay. (Source: Campaign Legal Center, "The STOCK Act: The Failed Effort to Stop Insider Trading in Congress")

The bill went nowhere. Slaughter and Baird reintroduced it in the 110th Congress (2007) and 111th Congress (2009). It never gained more than 14 co-sponsors in any session.

### The 60 Minutes Catalyst

Everything changed on November 13, 2011. CBS' "60 Minutes" aired a segment titled "Insiders" based on Peter Schweizer's book *Throw Them All Out*. Correspondent Steve Kroft confronted several members of Congress with evidence that they had traded stocks while holding material nonpublic information about pending legislation.

The segment featured Rep. Spencer Bachus (R-AL), then-ranking member of the House Financial Services Committee, who bought stock option funds that would profit from a market crash just days after receiving classified briefings from Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson and Fed Chairman Ben Bernanke about the impending 2008 financial crisis.

Before the segment aired, the STOCK Act had 9 co-sponsors. Within weeks, it had over 200. (Source: Campaign Legal Center)

### Passage

In the 112th Congress, Sen. Joe Lieberman (I-CT) introduced the Senate version (S. 2038) on January 26, 2012. The bill moved fast:

- **February 2, 2012:** Senate passed it 96-3. The three "no" votes: Jeff Bingaman (D-NM), Richard Burr (R-NC), and Tom Coburn (R-OK). (Source: GovTrack, Senate Vote #14)
- **February 9, 2012:** House passed its version 417-2. (Source: GovTrack, House Vote #47)
- **March 22, 2012:** House concurred in the Senate amendment.
- **April 4, 2012:** President Obama signed it into law as Public Law 112-105. (Source: congress.gov)

Notable: Richard Burr, who voted against the STOCK Act, was later investigated by the SEC and DOJ in 2020 for selling hundreds of thousands of dollars in stock shortly before the COVID-19 market crash, after receiving classified Senate briefings about the pandemic's severity. The investigation was dropped without charges. (Source: Washington Post, March 20, 2020)

### Key Provisions of the Act

1. **Affirmed insider trading law applies to government officials.** Section 4 states that "Members of Congress and employees of Congress are not exempt from the insider trading prohibitions" under Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Rule 10b-5. Section 9 extends this to executive branch employees, judicial officers, and judicial employees. (Source: Pub. L. 112-105, Sections 4 and 9)

2. **Established a "duty of trust and confidence."** Members of Congress, their staff, and all federal officials owe a duty to the U.S. government and its citizens regarding "material, nonpublic information" derived from their positions.

3. **Required periodic transaction reports.** Amended the Ethics in Government Act to require public financial disclosure filers to report securities transactions over $1,000 within 30 days of notification, but no later than 45 days after the transaction. (Source: 5 U.S.C. Section 13104(a); 5 U.S.C. Section 13105(l))

4. **Required online posting.** Financial disclosures were to be posted online in searchable, sortable databases. (This was largely gutted in 2013 -- see Section 8.)

5. **Prohibited officials from receiving favorable terms on IPOs.** [NEEDS VERIFICATION on exact section]

6. **Required disclosure of mortgage terms.** [NEEDS VERIFICATION on exact section]

## 2. Statutory Requirements: The Filing Timeline

The periodic transaction reporting requirement is codified at **5 U.S.C. Section 13104(a)(5)(B)** (formerly Section 103 of the Ethics in Government Act, before the 2022 recodification). The filing timeline is set by **5 U.S.C. Section 13105(l)**.

### The 30/45-Day Rule

The law creates a two-deadline system:

- **30 days after notification:** A filer must submit their Periodic Transaction Report (OGE Form 278-T) within 30 days of receiving notification of the transaction. "Notification" means written communication that a transaction has occurred -- an email from a broker, a trade confirmation, a monthly account statement.

- **45 days after the transaction itself:** Regardless of when the filer claims to have received notification, the report is due no later than 45 days after the transaction date. This is the hard backstop. (Source: 5 U.S.C. Section 13105(l))

### Who Must File

Anyone who files an OGE Form 278e (the annual public financial disclosure) must also file periodic transaction reports. This includes:

- The President and Vice President
- Cabinet secretaries and agency heads
- Senior Executive Service members at or above GS-15 or equivalent
- Presidential appointees confirmed by the Senate (PAS)
- Other senior officials designated by their agency's ethics office

### Spouse and Dependent Children

The requirement covers transactions by the filer, their spouse, and their dependent children. A filer is responsible for reporting their spouse's stock trades even if the spouse manages their own portfolio. (Source: OGE Form 278-T instructions)

## 3. What Must Be Disclosed -- and What's Exempt

### Covered Transactions (Must Report on 278-T)

Any purchase, sale, or exchange exceeding **$1,000** in:

- Individual stocks
- Corporate bonds
- Commodity futures
- Other securities (options, etc.)
- Transactions within defined contribution retirement plans (401(k), IRA) if in individual securities
- Transactions within variable annuities or 529 plans if in individual securities
- Transactions made by an investment advisor or account manager on the filer's behalf

### Amount Ranges

Transactions are reported in ranges, not exact dollar amounts. The statutory ranges are:

| Range |
|-------|
| $1,001 - $15,000 |
| $15,001 - $50,000 |
| $50,001 - $100,000 |
| $100,001 - $250,000 |
| $250,001 - $500,000 |
| $500,001 - $1,000,000 |
| $1,000,001 - $5,000,000 |
| $5,000,001 - $25,000,000 |
| $25,000,001 - $50,000,000 |
| Over $50,000,000 |

### Exempt Transactions (Not Required on 278-T)

1. **Mutual funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs)** -- All mutual funds and ETFs are classified as "excepted investment funds" (EIFs) and are exempt from periodic reporting. This is the single biggest loophole in the STOCK Act.
2. **U.S. Treasury bills, notes, and bonds** -- Government securities are exempt.
3. **Certificates of deposit, savings accounts, checking accounts, money market accounts**
4. **Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) accounts** -- The federal employee retirement system.
5. **Real property** -- Buying or selling a house or land does not require a 278-T.

### Qualified Blind Trusts

Assets placed in a Qualified Blind Trust (QBT) under 5 U.S.C. Section 13104(f)(3) are reported differently. Once assets are in a QBT, the filer does not know what the trustee buys or sells, so transaction reporting is not applicable for the trust's internal trades. The trust must be approved by the filer's supervising ethics office and meet specific requirements (independent trustee, no communication about holdings, etc.).

## 4. The Ethics in Government Act of 1978

### Background

The Ethics in Government Act (Pub. L. 95-521) was passed on October 26, 1978, in direct response to the Watergate scandal and the Saturday Night Massacre. It was the first comprehensive federal ethics law.

### What It Established

1. **Mandatory public financial disclosure** for senior government officials, including the President, Vice President, members of Congress, federal judges, and senior executive branch employees.

2. **The Office of Government Ethics (OGE)** -- Created to provide "overall direction of executive branch policies related to preventing conflicts of interest." OGE was initially housed within the Office of Personnel Management (OPM).

3. **Post-employment restrictions** -- Limits on lobbying by former officials for a set period after leaving government.

4. **The independent counsel mechanism** -- Established the special prosecutor/independent counsel provisions (later expired in 1999). [NEEDS VERIFICATION on exact expiration]

### OGE Becomes Independent (1988-1989)

The Office of Government Ethics Reauthorization Act of 1988 (Pub. L. 100-598) separated OGE from OPM, making it an independent executive branch agency effective October 1, 1989. (Source: Federal Register; OGE, "Implementation of the OGE Reauthorization Act of 1988," 55 FR 1665)

### The 2022 Recodification

In 2022, the Ethics in Government Act was recodified from Title 5 Appendix into the main body of Title 5 (Chapter 131). The old Section 101-112 numbering became Sections 13101-13111. The substance did not change -- only the section numbers.

## 5. 18 U.S.C. Section 208: The Criminal Conflict-of-Interest Statute

### What It Does

Section 208 prohibits any federal employee from participating "personally and substantially" in any "particular matter" in which they, their spouse, minor child, general partner, or certain affiliated organizations have a financial interest. (Source: 18 U.S.C. Section 208; Cornell LII)

### How It Differs from the STOCK Act

| | STOCK Act | 18 U.S.C. Section 208 |
|---|---|---|
| **What it regulates** | Trading on inside information | Taking official action while conflicted |
| **Trigger** | Buying/selling securities using nonpublic info | Participating in a matter affecting your financial interest |
| **Disclosure focus** | Transaction reports (after-the-fact) | Recusal/divestiture (before acting) |
| **Enforcement** | $200 late fee; SEC insider trading prosecution | Criminal prosecution by DOJ |

### Penalties

- **Willful violation:** Felony. Up to 5 years in prison. (Source: 18 U.S.C. Section 208(a))
- **Non-willful violation:** Misdemeanor. Up to 1 year in prison.
- **Civil penalties:** Up to $50,000 per violation and/or injunction. (Source: 18 U.S.C. Section 216)

## 6. 26 U.S.C. Section 1043: The Certificate of Divestiture Tax Break

### Purpose

When a federal employee is told to sell assets to comply with conflict-of-interest requirements, they may face a huge capital gains tax bill. Section 1043 exists so that the tax burden does not deter qualified people from entering government service. Enacted as part of the Ethics Reform Act of 1989 (Pub. L. 101-194).

### How It Works

1. The employee requests a Certificate of Divestiture from OGE.
2. OGE issues the certificate, confirming the sale is required to comply with ethics rules.
3. The employee sells the conflicting asset.
4. Within 60 days, the employee must reinvest the proceeds in "permitted property."
5. Capital gains tax is deferred -- not eliminated -- until the permitted property is eventually sold.

### Permitted Property

- U.S. Treasury obligations (bills, notes, bonds)
- Diversified investment funds approved by OGE (typically broad-market mutual funds or ETFs)

## 7. Penalties for STOCK Act Violations

### The $200 Late Filing Fee

Under 5 U.S.C. Section 13106(a), any individual who files a required report more than 30 days after the due date "shall pay a filing fee of $200." The fee covers all transactions that could have been timely included in a single report.

### Waiver Authority

The supervising ethics office "may waive the filing fee under this subsection in extraordinary circumstances." OGE issued a blanket waiver when the STOCK Act first took effect; it expired July 3, 2013. After that, waivers became case-by-case.

### Civil Penalties

The Attorney General may bring a civil action against anyone who "knowingly and willfully falsifies or who knowingly and willfully fails to file or report any information." Civil penalties can reach up to $50,000. (Source: 5 U.S.C. Section 13106(b))

### Criminal Penalties

Any person who "knowingly and willfully" falsifies or fails to file may be imprisoned for not more than 1 year. (Source: 5 U.S.C. Section 13106(c))

False statements can also be prosecuted under 18 U.S.C. Section 1001, which carries up to 5 years.

### Insider Trading Penalties

Because the STOCK Act affirmed that insider trading laws apply to government officials:

- **Criminal:** Up to 20 years imprisonment and fines up to $5 million for individuals.
- **Civil:** Up to three times the profit gained or loss avoided.

### Enforcement Reality

No member of Congress or executive branch official has ever been criminally prosecuted under the STOCK Act's insider trading provisions. The $200 late filing fee is the only penalty regularly applied, and even that is inconsistently enforced.

## 8. The 2013 Rollback: S. 716

On April 11, 2013, the Senate passed S. 716 by unanimous consent. On April 12, 2013, the House did the same -- after only 14 seconds of floor debate. President Obama signed it on April 15, 2013.

### What S. 716 Repealed

- The requirement that financial disclosures filed by congressional staff and executive branch employees (below President/VP/Congress) be posted online
- The requirement to create searchable, sortable databases
- The requirement for electronic filing

### What Was NOT Repealed

The 45-day transaction reporting requirement itself remained intact. Disclosures by the President, Vice President, members of Congress, and congressional candidates remained available online.

### Practical Effect

This is why Open Cabinet's data pipeline requires going through the OGE API and Form 201 requests rather than simply scraping a public database. For Level I and Level II officials, disclosures are still posted. For thousands of mid-level officials, public access requires individual written requests via OGE Form 201.

## 9. The Forms: OGE 278, 278e, and 278-T

### OGE Form 278e (Current Annual Disclosure)

Replaced the old Form 278 in January 2016. The "e" stands for electronic. Comprehensive annual financial disclosure covering assets, income, liabilities, and transactions.

### OGE Form 278-T (Periodic Transaction Report)

Created by the STOCK Act. Filed on a rolling basis. For each covered transaction exceeding $1,000:

- **Description:** The asset name, often with ticker symbol
- **Type:** Sale, Purchase, Sale (Partial), Sale (Full), Exchange
- **Date:** The date the transaction occurred
- **Notification Received Over 30 Days Ago:** Yes or No (the late filing flag)
- **Amount:** Reported in statutory ranges

### OGE Form 201 (Request for Access)

The form the public uses to request a copy of a specific official's financial disclosure. For officials whose filings are not posted online, the Form 201 is the only way to access their records.
