# STOCK Act Late Filing Patterns: A Research Brief

## For the Open Cabinet Project -- Executive Branch Financial Transaction Tracker

*Compiled April 12, 2026*

## 1. What Constitutes a "Late Filing"

The Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act of 2012 (Public Law 112-105) requires that any covered official who executes a securities transaction exceeding $1,000 must file a Periodic Transaction Report (OGE Form 278-T) disclosing that transaction. The statute provides a dual deadline:

- **30 days** after the filer receives notification of the transaction; OR
- **45 days** after the transaction itself, whichever comes first.

(Source: [Department of Energy STOCK Act guidance](https://www.energy.gov/gc/stop-trading-congressional-knowledge-stock-act-periodic-transaction-reporting-requirements-oge); [USDA STOCK Act memo](https://www.usda.gov/sites/default/files/documents/OE%20Stock%20Act%20memo--Transactions.pdf))

### The Self-Certification Field

Each OGE Form 278-T includes a column labeled **"Notification Received More Than 30 Days Ago"** that filers must mark Yes or No for every listed transaction. A "Yes" answer is effectively a self-admission that the filer is reporting late -- the form itself creates the paper trail. This field is critical for Open Cabinet's data because it appears in every parsed PDF and allows programmatic identification of late disclosures without needing to calculate filing dates independently.

(Source: [OGE Form 278-T (2019 edition)](https://ethics.od.nih.gov/sites/default/files/Minutes/PDF-2019/OGE%20Form%20278_Jan2019_FINAL%20Fillable%20PDF%20(1).pdf); [DOD SOCO 278-T Tips](https://dodsoco.ogc.osd.mil/Portals/102/oge_278t_tips_1.pdf))

### Extension Provisions

An agency ethics official may grant an extension of up to 45 days for "good cause shown" if the filer received notification more than 30 days before filing. Extensions can be granted after the fact. The Office of Government Ethics Legal Advisory 12-04 clarified that there is broad discretion to grant extensions and waivers for OGE Form 278-T reports.

(Source: [DOD SOCO Financial Disclosure Deskbook](https://dodsoco.ogc.osd.mil/Portals/102/financial_disclosure_program%20(1).pdf))

## 2. Enforcement: The $200 Late Fee

### The Statutory Penalty

Section 104(d) of the Ethics in Government Act (codified at 5 U.S.C. Section 13106) imposes a **$200 late filing fee** on any reporting individual who files a public financial disclosure report more than 30 days after the statutory deadline (including any extension period). The fee is payable to the U.S. Treasury.

(Source: [5 CFR 2634.704 -- Late filing fee](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-5/chapter-XVI/subchapter-B/part-2634/subpart-G/section-2634.704))

### Who Assesses It

For executive branch filers, the fee is assessed by the filer's agency Designated Agency Ethics Official (DAEO). For members of Congress, the House Committee on Ethics and the Senate Select Committee on Ethics handle enforcement for their respective chambers.

### Waiver Provisions

The ethics committees may waive the $200 fee, but only in "extraordinary circumstances." In practice, waivers appear to be routine. When Rep. Julia Letlow disclosed more than 210 late stock and bond trades in early 2026, the House Ethics Committee's director of financial disclosure agreed to waive the $200 penalty after she requested it.

(Source: [NOTUS, "Rep. Julia Letlow Violated the STOCK Act With Over 210 Late Disclosures," January 2026](https://www.notus.org/congress/julia-letlow-stock-act-disclosures))

### Collection Reality

No public records exist to indicate whether members of Congress who are fined actually pay the $200 fee. The Campaign Legal Center has noted that "no public records exist to indicate whether members of Congress who are fined even pay."

For executive branch officials, agency debt collection procedures may be utilized, including salary offset or administrative action. But the Department of Justice does not pursue $200 late fee claims, as there is no cause of action under Section 104 of the Ethics in Government Act for the DOJ to enforce.

(Source: [Campaign Legal Center, "The STOCK Act: The Failed Effort to Stop Insider Trading in Congress," Part 2](https://campaignlegal.org/update/part-2-stock-act-failed-effort-stop-insider-trading-congress))

### The Deterrence Problem

The penalty is $200 per late report -- not per transaction. A single 278-T can contain dozens of transactions. Rep. Letlow disclosed 224 late transactions across multiple reports and the total penalty exposure was the same $200 that could be (and was) waived. For officials whose transactions are worth millions, $200 is not a meaningful deterrent.

## 3. Congressional Late Filing Data

### Business Insider's "Conflicted Congress" Investigation (2021-2023)

Business Insider launched its "Conflicted Congress" project in December 2021, rating every member of Congress on financial conflicts. The investigation identified at least **48 members of Congress and 182 senior-level congressional staffers** who violated the STOCK Act as of December 2021. By 2022, the count had risen to at least **72 members** of Congress caught violating the law.

(Source: [Insider on X, December 2021](https://x.com/thisisinsider/status/1470512028334936073); [Zach Nunn for Congress citing Insider reporting, 2022](https://zachnunn.com/axne72-members-of-congress-caught-violating-law-on-stock-trades/))

### Campaign Legal Center Complaints

The Campaign Legal Center has been the most persistent outside enforcer of the STOCK Act. Key actions:

- **September 2021:** Filed complaints against **7 House members** (4 Democrats, 3 Republicans) for failing to disclose stock trades. Rep. Tom Suozzi (D-NY) alone failed to file reports on approximately **300 transactions**. Five of the seven sat on the House Financial Services Committee.
- **Through 2024:** CLC had filed **15 total complaints** representing between **$14.3 million and $52.1 million** in undisclosed or untimely disclosed stock trading.
- **2024:** Filed a complaint against Rep. Byron Donalds (R-FL) for failing to disclose over **100 stock transactions** worth up to $1.6 million.

A 2022 CLC analysis found the House Ethics Committee investigated only **8 of 60 potential violations** since 2020 and "effectively dismissed all of the cases."

(Source: [NPR, "Ethics Group: 7 House Members Are Violating The STOCK Act," September 22, 2021](https://www.npr.org/2021/09/22/1039287987/outside-ethics-group-says-7-house-lawmakers-didnt-disclose-stock-trades); [Campaign Legal Center, "Congressional Stock Trading Continues to Raise Conflicts of Interest Concerns"](https://campaignlegal.org/update/congressional-stock-trading-continues-raise-conflicts-interest-concerns); [Sludge, August 16, 2024](https://readsludge.com/2024/08/16/fetterman-gallego-and-others-violate-ethics-law-by-failing-to-report-stock-trades/))

### Notable Individual Cases

**Rep. Julia Letlow (R-LA):** Failed to disclose **224 stock and bond trades** within the 45-day window, worth between $225,000 and $3.3 million. Some were disclosed over a year late. Her office blamed a discretionary investment firm. The House Ethics Committee waived her $200 penalty. (Source: [NOTUS, January 2026](https://www.notus.org/congress/julia-letlow-stock-act-disclosures))

**Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA):** Reported **31 transactions** from 2023 about 17 months after the filing deadline, including corporate bonds and a Marathon Petroleum stock sale. His office blamed grandparents who created investment accounts for his children without knowing the reporting requirements. (Source: [Sludge, August 16, 2024](https://readsludge.com/2024/08/16/fetterman-gallego-and-others-violate-ethics-law-by-failing-to-report-stock-trades/))

**Rep. Ruben Gallego (D-AZ):** Disclosed two transactions nearly **two and five years** late, worth between $15,001 and $50,000 each. (Source: [Sludge, August 16, 2024](https://readsludge.com/2024/08/16/fetterman-gallego-and-others-violate-ethics-law-by-failing-to-report-stock-trades/))

**August 2024 snapshot:** In a single month, **9 lawmakers** reported more than **125 unreported transactions** worth up to $2.6 million total. Rep. Greg Landsman (D-OH) alone disclosed 86 transactions dating back to January 2023, in stocks including Nvidia, Amazon, and Microsoft. (Source: [Sludge, August 16, 2024](https://readsludge.com/2024/08/16/fetterman-gallego-and-others-violate-ethics-law-by-failing-to-report-stock-trades/))

### Scale of Congressional Trading

- In 2023, members of Congress made approximately **11,000 trades** across both chambers.
- 44% of House members and 54% of Senate members own individual stocks.
- A New York Times investigation found that from 2019 to 2021, **18% of members** traded stocks in sectors related to committees they sat on.

(Source: [Campaign Legal Center, 2024](https://campaignlegal.org/update/congressional-stock-trading-continues-raise-conflicts-interest-concerns); [Brennan Center for Justice, "Congressional Stock Trading, Explained"](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/congressional-stock-trading-explained))

## 4. Executive Branch Late Filing Patterns

### Open Cabinet Project Data

Our own parsed data from OGE 278-T filings for 29 current executive branch officials reveals a significant late filing problem:

- **563 of 2,118 transactions (26.6%)** are self-certified as late -- meaning the filer marked "Notification Received More Than 30 Days Ago" as Yes on the 278-T form.
- **Donald Trump** leads with **213 of 389 transactions** flagged late (54.8%).
- **Linda McMahon** (Education Secretary): **144 of 147** transactions late (98%).
- **Doug Burgum** (Interior Secretary): **103 of 124** late (83%).
- **Troy Edgar** (Deputy DHS Secretary): **63 of 63** late (100%).

These numbers come from self-certification on the 278-T forms. They represent cases where the filer acknowledged receiving trade notification more than 30 days before filing. The actual late rate may be higher, since some filers may not accurately self-report.

### OGE's Limited Oversight

The Office of Government Ethics does not publish aggregate compliance statistics for periodic transaction reports across the executive branch. OGE's FY2023 annual report noted that more than **29,000 public reports** and **420,000 confidential reports** were filed that year, but did not break out on-time vs. late filing rates.

(Source: [OGE FY2023 Annual Performance Report](https://www.oge.gov/web/OGE.nsf/0/BB5FF70AAC120E0885258ADD006F777A/$FILE/U.S.%20Office%20of%20Government%20Ethics%202023%20APR%20(publication).pdf))

### Notable Executive Branch Compliance Failures

**Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent:** Missed his April 28, 2025 divestiture deadline for Key Square Group hedge fund assets. OGE informed the Senate Finance Committee on August 11, 2025 that Bessent had failed to comply. He was given an extension to December 15, 2025, and completed his final divestiture (a soybean farm) in early December 2025 -- roughly 10 months past the original deadline.

(Source: [Bloomberg, "Bessent Given Extension to Divest After Missing Ethics Deadline," August 13, 2025](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-13/bessent-warned-on-ethics-compliance-granted-extension-to-divest); [CNBC, "Treasury Secretary Bessent says he has divested his soybean farm," December 7, 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/07/treasury-secretary-bessent-divested-soybean-farm.html))

**FAA Administrator Bryan Bedford:** Held **$5 million to $26 million** in Republic Airways Holdings stock for over seven months after taking office, despite signing an ethics agreement to divest within 90 days of his July 9, 2025 confirmation. The OGE rejected his request for a 60-day extension. He finally disclosed the sale in March 2026. The Project on Government Oversight called the delay "concerning."

(Source: [NOTUS, "FAA Chief Dumps Millions in Airline Stock Months After His Ethics Agreement Deadline"](https://www.notus.org/money/bryan-bedford-faa-republic-stock-sale-aviation); [Senate Commerce Committee, December 2025](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/12/faa-administrator-bedford-in-violation-of-ethics-agreement-potentially-holds-millions-in-republic-airways-stock-months-after-confirmation-committee))

**White House Staff Disclosures:** A March 2026 NOTUS investigation found that at least **5 top White House officials** -- including Border Czar Tom Homan and House liaison Jeffrey Freeland -- had no financial disclosures available in OGE's database, months after they were required. Nine officials submitted disclosures to OGE in early January 2026, **over six months** after White House reviews apparently finished. Will Scharf, the Staff Secretary, made 14 stock trades from June through November 2025, but his public disclosure was delayed until late January 2026.

(Source: [NOTUS, "The White House's Financial Disclosures for Top Officials Are Incomplete," March 10, 2026](https://www.notus.org/trump-white-house/white-houses-financial-disclosures-top-officials-incomplete))

### OGE's Enforcement Posture

In April 2025, OGE wrote to the White House establishing deadlines for disclosure certification and noting it could decline to certify reports for reasons including unresolved potentially conflicting holdings or non-compliance with ethics rules. But OGE has "little power to enforce ethics policies" -- it can embarrass but cannot fine or prosecute. The Campaign Legal Center and Demand for Disclosure have demanded investigations into Bessent's potential conflicts, but no enforcement action has resulted.

(Source: [Campaign Legal Center, "Ethics Watchdogs Demand Investigations into Treasury Secretary Bessent's Potential Conflicts of Interest," 2025](https://campaignlegal.org/press-releases/ethics-watchdogs-demand-investigations-treasury-secretary-scott-bessents-potential))

## 5. Criminal Prosecution History

### Has Anyone Been Criminally Prosecuted Under the STOCK Act?

**No.** No member of Congress or executive branch official has ever been criminally prosecuted specifically under the STOCK Act. The act expanded insider trading prohibitions to cover government officials, but every actual prosecution has used existing securities law.

(Source: [Campaign Legal Center, "The STOCK Act: The Failed Effort to Stop Insider Trading in Congress"](https://campaignlegal.org/update/stock-act-failed-effort-stop-insider-trading-congress); [Georgetown Law, "Failures of the STOCK Act and the Future of Congressional Insider Trading Jurisprudence," 2021](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/american-criminal-law-review/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2021/05/58-0-Mesiya-Failures-of-the-Stock-Act-UPDATED.pdf))

### Rep. Chris Collins (R-NY) -- Insider Trading Conviction

Collins was charged in August 2018 not under the STOCK Act but under **Rule 10(b)(5) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934**, plus wire fraud and making false statements to the FBI. The crime: while attending a Congressional picnic at the White House on June 22, 2017, Collins received nonpublic information that Innate Immunotherapeutics' drug had failed a clinical trial. He immediately tipped off his son Cameron, who sold approximately **1.39 million shares**, avoiding approximately **$570,900 in losses**.

Collins pleaded guilty in October 2019. Sentenced to **26 months** in federal prison on January 17, 2020, by Judge Vernon S. Broderick. Began serving at FPC Pensacola on October 13, 2020. **Pardoned by President Trump on December 22, 2020** -- roughly two months into his sentence. As of January 2026, Collins was reportedly seeking a political comeback in Florida.

(Source: [DOJ, Southern District of New York, sentencing announcement](https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/pr/former-congressman-christopher-collins-sentenced-insider-trading-scheme-and-lying); [CNBC, January 17, 2020](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/01/17/chris-collins-sentenced-to-26-months-for-insider-trading-tip.html); [Roll Call, January 23, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/01/23/former-new-york-rep-chris-collins-pardoned-by-trump-seeks-comeback-in-florida/))

### The 2020 Pandemic Trading Scandal

In February-March 2020, shortly after receiving classified Senate briefings on the COVID-19 threat, several senators made large stock sales:

- **Sen. Richard Burr (R-NC):** Sold between $628,000 and $1.72 million in stocks. The FBI served a search warrant on his phone in May 2020.
- **Sen. Kelly Loeffler (R-GA):** Her household made 27 transactions selling $1.275 million to $3.1 million in stock, while purchasing shares of Citrix Systems (a remote-work company).
- **Sen. Dianne Feinstein (D-CA):** Sold Allogene Therapeutics stock estimated between $1.5 million and $6 million.
- **Sen. James Inhofe (R-OK):** Also under investigation for stock sales.

The DOJ closed investigations into Feinstein, Loeffler, and Inhofe on **May 26, 2020**, citing insufficient evidence. The Burr investigation continued longer but was ultimately closed on **January 19, 2021** -- the last day of the Trump administration -- without charges.

Prosecutors faced constitutional hurdles unique to Congress. The **Speech or Debate Clause** (Article I, Section 6) shields legislative activities from investigation, creating a nearly impenetrable defense for lawmakers who receive information through committee briefings.

(Source: [NPR, "Insider-Trading Probes Of Feinstein, Loeffler, Inhofe Closed," May 26, 2020](https://www.npr.org/2020/05/26/862692569/justice-department-closes-investigations-of-3-senators-burr-inquiry-continues); [CNN, May 22, 2020](https://www.cnn.com/2020/05/22/politics/richard-burr-prosecutors-stocks/index.html); [Washington Post, May 26, 2020](https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/legal-issues/justice-dept-ends-coronavirus-insider-trading-investigations-into-us-sens-loeffler-inhofe-and-feinstein/2020/05/26/5e59b9a4-9f8b-11ea-b5c9-570a91917d8d_story.html))

## 6. GAO and Inspector General Findings

### GAO-25-107039: "Financial Disclosure: Updates Are Needed to the Public Reporting Requirements" (December 2024)

This is the most recent GAO report on executive branch financial disclosure. Key findings:

- Studies spanning **25 years** found that statutory reporting requirements for public filers are **"outdated, inconsistent, and, in some cases, unnecessary."**
- OGE's last evaluation of the disclosure program was in **2005**. Congress had not updated requirements as of October 2024.
- Recommendations called for: raising reporting thresholds, reducing the number of value categories, ensuring consistency across regulations, and eliminating disclosure of unnecessary information.
- Hundreds of thousands of federal employees file annual disclosure reports, but the GAO found the system is designed around a framework that hasn't been modernized in decades.

(Source: [GAO-25-107039](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107039))

### The 2013 STOCK Act Rollback

The single most consequential post-enactment change to the STOCK Act occurred on **April 15, 2013**, when President Obama signed S.716, which eliminated the requirement for approximately **28,000 government officials** to post financial information online in a searchable, sortable database.

The bill received **14 seconds** of debate on the House floor before passing by unanimous consent. The justification: a National Academy of Public Administration report warned that online posting could endanger government employees abroad and hamper recruitment.

The rollback preserved online posting for the President, Vice President, members of Congress, and Cabinet-level officials (Executive Level I and II). But it gutted the searchable database requirement for the vast majority of executive branch filers, making systematic oversight far more difficult.

(Source: [NPR, "How Congress Quietly Overhauled Its Insider-Trading Law," April 16, 2013](https://www.npr.org/sections/itsallpolitics/2013/04/16/177496734/how-congress-quietly-overhauled-its-insider-trading-law); [S.716, 113th Congress](https://www.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/senate-bill/716))

### OGE Agency Review Activity

OGE conducts periodic reviews of individual agency ethics programs but does not publish a government-wide compliance rate for periodic transaction reports. In FY2023, OGE reported:

- **100% agency compliance** with the Annual Agency Ethics Program Questionnaire (140+ agencies).
- Nearly **6,000 public inspection requests** processed, covering approximately 13,400 documents.
- **29 ethics officials from 24 agencies** completed the Advanced Certificate in Ethics program.

These numbers describe the infrastructure, not outcomes. There is no publicly available aggregate data showing what percentage of required periodic transaction reports are filed on time across the executive branch. ## 7. Reform Proposals

### TRUST in Congress Act

**Sponsors:** Rep. Abigail Spanberger (D-VA) and Rep. Chip Roy (R-TX), with 44 co-sponsors. Reintroduced in the 119th Congress. Companion Senate version from Sens. Ashley Moody (R-FL) and Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY).

**What it does:** Requires all members of Congress, their spouses, and dependent children to either **divest from individual stock holdings** or place them in a **qualified blind trust** during their entire tenure. Current members would have 180 days after enactment; new members would have 90 days after taking office.

**Covered investments:** Stocks, bonds, commodities, futures, and comparable economic interests acquired through synthetic means such as derivatives.

**Endorsed by:** Public Citizen, POGO, CREW, Protect Democracy, Americans for Prosperity, National Taxpayers Union, Fix the Court, Issue One, and others across the political spectrum.

(Source: [Rep. Spanberger press release, 2025](https://spanberger.house.gov/posts/at-start-of-new-congress-spanberger-roy-reintroduce-trust-in-congress-act-to-ban-members-of-congress-their-families-from-trading-individual-stocks); [Rep. Magaziner press release](https://magaziner.house.gov/media/press-releases/magaziner-roy-introduce-bipartisan-legislation-ban-members-congress-trading))

### ETHICS Act

**Sponsors:** Sens. Jeff Merkley (D-OR), Gary Peters (D-MI), Jon Ossoff (D-GA), and Josh Hawley (R-MO). Would ban members, their spouses, and dependent children from trading individual stocks.

**Sponsors:** Sens. Ashley Moody (R-FL) and Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY). Would ban stock ownership and trading for members and immediate family members.

Nearly all current proposals target Congress. ## 8. International Comparison

### United Kingdom

**Ministerial Code:** Ministers must provide their Permanent Secretary with a complete list of all financial interests. The **List of Ministers' Interests** is published periodically (most recent: October 2025) and includes interests "relevant, or may be perceived to be relevant, to their ministerial portfolio." It is not a register of all interests.

**Blind Trust Requirement:** Not explicitly required by the Ministerial Code. Rather, it is left to "the personal responsibility of each Minister to decide whether and what action is needed to avoid a conflict." Blind trusts and blind management arrangements are "longstanding mechanisms" available to ministers. The Prime Minister may direct a minister to divest, but enforcement is soft.

**Weakness:** Spotlight on Corruption, an anti-corruption group, has published a briefing questioning whether blind trusts are a "transparency blackhole" -- they solve the conflict problem by creating an accountability problem. No one can verify compliance.

(Source: [UK Government, "List of Ministers' Interests: October 2025"](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/list-of-ministers-interests/list-of-ministers-interests-october-2025-html); [Spotlight on Corruption, "Blind Trusts: integrity silver bullet or transparency blackhole?" May 2023](https://www.spotlightcorruption.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Briefing-Blind-Trusts-2023.pdf))

### Canada

**Conflict of Interest Act:** The strongest model among peer democracies. Ministers, ministers of state, and parliamentary secretaries **must divest all controlled assets** within **120 days** of appointment -- either through arm's-length sale or placement in a qualified blind trust. This is mandatory, not discretionary.

**Enforcement:** The **Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner** (an independent officer of Parliament) oversees compliance. The Commissioner must approve blind trust arrangements and can order divestiture. Trustees cannot share trust composition with the officeholder or take instructions from them.

**Key difference from U.S.:** Canada's system has an independent enforcement body with actual authority. In the U.S., OGE can advise and embarrass, but cannot compel. Canada's commissioner can publicly find a minister in violation.

(Source: [Canada Conflict of Interest Act, S.C. 2006](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/C-36.65/section-27.html); [Summary of Rules for Ministers, Government of Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/canadian-heritage/corporate/transparency/open-government/standing-committee/chagger-information-privacy-ethics/summary-rules-ministers.html); [CIEC Divestment and Blind Trusts guidance](https://ciec-ccie.parl.gc.ca/en/rules-reglements/Pages/INDivestmentBlindTrust-AIDessaisissementFiducies.aspx))

### Australia

**Statement of Ministerial Standards:** Ministers must declare and register personal interests, including pecuniary interests, with disclosures recorded in the **Ministerial Register of Interests** maintained by Cabinet Office.

**Divestiture:** The Prime Minister may direct a minister to divest any interest that could give rise to a conflict. In Queensland, ministers must divest any shareholding where a conflict "exists or could reasonably be perceived to exist." At the Commonwealth level, ministers must divest investments in any public or private company or business.

**Key limitation:** Transferring an interest to a family member or to a trust in which the minister has a beneficial interest does not count as divestment.

**Frequency:** South Australian ministers provide yearly updates to Parliament and Cabinet Office each August.

(Source: [Australian Statement of Ministerial Standards, August 2018](https://www.aph.gov.au/DocumentStore.ashx?id=cba0adb2-68c3-4d98-b3d7-61c1eb6f1652&subId=668917); [Queensland Ministerial Handbook](https://www.premiers.qld.gov.au/publications/categories/policies-and-codes/handbooks/ministerial-handbook/ethics/pecuniary.aspx); [South Australia Ministerial Code of Conduct](https://www.dpc.sa.gov.au/responsibilities/cabinet-and-executive-council/executive-government/ministerial-code-of-conduct))

### European Parliament

A May 2023 study by the European Parliament Research Service compared financial disclosure obligations across national parliaments worldwide. The study found that **the level of transparency varies widely**, with some countries providing rigorous and frequent disclosure requirements while others require minimal reporting. The study examined proposals to strengthen integrity, independence, and accountability rules for MEPs.

(Source: [European Parliament, "A comparative analysis of financial disclosure obligations on members of parliaments," May 2023](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/thinktank/en/document/EPRS_STU(2023)747911))

## Key Takeaways for Open Cabinet

1. **The self-certification field is gold.** The "Notification Received More Than 30 Days Ago" column on every 278-T is a filer's own admission of lateness. Our data shows 26.6% of executive branch transactions carry this flag -- a number that has never been aggregated and reported before, because nobody has systematically parsed these PDFs until now.

2. **The executive branch is a blind spot.** Congressional late filings get investigated by Business Insider, tracked by Unusual Whales, and flagged by the Campaign Legal Center. Executive branch periodic transaction compliance has no equivalent watchdog. Open Cabinet fills that gap.

3. **Enforcement is toothless everywhere.** The $200 penalty is routinely waived. No criminal prosecution has ever been brought under the STOCK Act. The DOJ will not pursue late fee claims. OGE cannot compel compliance. The system runs on shame, and shame requires someone watching.

4. **Canada provides the reform model.** A mandatory 120-day divestiture enforced by an independent commissioner with real authority is the strongest framework among peer democracies. The U.S. has the disclosure infrastructure but not the enforcement muscle.
